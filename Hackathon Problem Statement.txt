Background 
In 1934, British crossword compiler Edward Powys Mathers, writing under the pseudonym Torquemada, published an extraordinary literary puzzle titled Cain’s Jawbone.
The book consisted of 100 pages. Completely out of order. Readers were challenged to rearrange them correctly and, in doing so, identify six victims and their respective murderers hidden within the narrative.
Despite being nearly a century old, the puzzle remains notoriously difficult. For decades, only two people were known to have solved it correctly after its original publication. In recent years, a small number of additional solvers have emerged, but the total remains extremely low given the number of attempts worldwide. The solution itself is still treated with a degree of secrecy, and the puzzle continues to be regarded as one of the most challenging literary enigmas ever created.
What makes it so difficult is not just the disorder, but the subtlety of the clues. The correct sequence can only be inferred through:
linguistic continuity
character and object tracking
temporal and causal reasoning
careful interpretation of nuanced text
Motivation
Inspired by this historic challenge, we ask:
Can machines reconstruct a narrative when its structure has been completely disrupted?
In many real-world scenarios, information is fragmented. Logs are shuffled. Documents lose ordering. Context must be inferred. This problem mirrors those challenges in a controlled setting.
You are placed in the role of an archivist tasked with restoring a lost narrative from disordered fragments.

Problem Statement
You are given collections of text fragments (“pages”) from mystery narratives. The original ordering has been removed.
Your task is to:
Reconstruct the correct sequence of pages using algorithmic or machine learning techniques.
No explicit ordering cues are provided. The solution must emerge from understanding the text.

Evaluation
Submissions will be evaluated using a Kendall Tau-based score, which measures pairwise ordering correctness.
Let:
D = number of incorrectly ordered page pairs
T = n(n − 1) / 2 = total number of pairs
The Kendall Tau coefficient is:
τ = 1 − (2D / T)
The final normalized score is:
Score = (τ + 1) / 2
The final score is normalized to the range [0, 1]

Final Score
Final Score = 0.5 × Score(BookA) + 0.5 × Score(BookB)
